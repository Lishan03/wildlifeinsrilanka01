ANIMALS IN SRI LANKA

Sri Lanka, a biodiverse island nation in South Asia, boasts a varied wildlife population thriving in its diverse ecosystems. From the freely roaming Sri Lankan elephants in national parks to elusive leopards navigating dense jungles, the country is a haven for wildlife enthusiasts. Tree-dwelling primates like langurs and macaques, alongside vibrant bird species such as the Sri Lanka junglefowl, add to the island's ecological richness. The rivers and coastal waters host diverse aquatic life, including saltwater crocodiles, sea turtles, and various fish species. Sri Lanka's commitment to conservation, evident in its network of protected areas, plays a crucial role in safeguarding the captivating array of animal life found throughout the nation.

Purple faced langur

The purple-faced langur, also known as the purple-faced leaf monkey,ispecies of Old World monkey that is endemic to Sri Lanka. The animal is a long-tailed arboreal species, identified by a mostly brown appearance,dark face and a very shy nature.

Sri Lankan Elephant

Sri Lankan Elephant | Species | WWF The Sri Lankan subspecies is the largest and also the darkest of the Asian elephants, with patches of depigmentation—areas with no skin color—on its ears, face, trunk and belly.

Solth Bear

Sri Lankan Sloth Bear | Cinnamon Nature Trails Sri Lanka Yala, Wilpattu, Wasgomuwa National Parks are the most popular places to watch Sloth Bears. Expert tree climbers in search of berries.

Sri Lankan junglefowl

Female Sri Lanka junglefowl are drab in color and smaller than males. Females lack a fleshy comb and wattles, but have a small crest at the base of the beak. The wattles and comb of the male Sri Lanka junglefowl are bright red. There is a distinctive yellow patch in the center of his comb.

Red slender loris

The red slender loris is a small species of primate endemic to Sri Lanka. It is nocturnal and inhabits lowland rainforests, up to 700 m / 2,300 ft in altitude.

Russell's Viper

venomous snake in the family Viperidae native to the Indian subcontinent and one of the big four snakes in India. It was described in 1797 by George Shaw and Frederick Polydore Nodder, and named after Patrick Russell, who wrote about it in his 1796 work An account of Indian serpents, collected on the coast of Coromandel.

Macaques

Macaques (Macaca spp.) are Old World monkeys, with the greatest geographical distribution of all non-human primates, across Asia, Southern Europe and North Africa. There are 22 species; the two most commonly used in research and testing are the cynomolgus macaque (Macaca fascicularis) and the rhesus macaque (Macaca mulatta).

Sambar deer

The large deer native to the Indian subcontinent, South China and Southeast Asia that is listed as a vulnerable species on the IUCN Red List since 2008. Populations have declined substantially due to severe hunting, local insurgency, and industrial exploitation of habitat.
